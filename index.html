ABOUT

Nestled in the heart of the Cordillera Mountains in Ifugao Province, Banaue is home to one of humanity’s greatest engineering achievements. Often called the “Eighth Wonder of the World,” these terraces were hand-carved over 2,000 years ago by the Ifugao people, long before modern tools or technology existed. The terraces stretch across the mountains like giant steps reaching toward the clouds, creating a stunning landscape that blends nature, culture, and ingenuity. They are a living testament to the harmony between people and the land. Each layer of the terraces tells a story of community, faith, and perseverance. The Ifugao built them not only to grow rice but to honor their ancestors and the spirits that guard the earth. Water from mountain streams flows through an ancient irrigation system that still works today, keeping the terraces alive through generations.

Recognized by UNESCO as part of the Rice Terraces of the Philippine Cordilleras World Heritage Site, Banaue stands as a symbol of sustainable living and cultural pride. The people of Ifugao continue to preserve this sacred landscape, keeping their traditions alive while sharing their story with the world. Whether you come to witness its breathtaking views, listen to the songs of the Hudhud, or meet the people whose ancestors shaped these mountains, Banaue invites you to experience a heritage carved in time.

Meet the

PEOPLE

High in the Cordillera Mountains live the Ifugao people, the proud builders and caretakers of the Banaue Rice Terraces. For more than 2,000 years, their ancestors carved the mountainsides with simple hand tools, turning steep slopes into a breathtaking system of rice paddies that still feeds their communities today.

The Ifugao are known for their strong sense of unity, respect for nature, and deep spirituality. Every part of their daily life — from planting rice to celebrating harvest — is guided by rituals and traditions that honor their ancestors and the spirits of the land. Their way of life reflects the belief that humans, earth, and the divine must remain in balance.

Explore the

LAND

Ifugao villages are close-knit and family-centered. Homes are built on sturdy wooden posts with thatched roofs, symbolizing strength and resilience. Farming is still done mostly by hand, using methods passed down through generations.

Aside from rice cultivation, the Ifugao are also skilled weavers and woodcarvers, known for crafting the Bulul (rice guardian figure) and the Hagabi (prestige bench), both important symbols of status and spirituality.

Know the

ROOTS

Spirituality runs deep in Ifugao life. Rituals called baki are performed by a Mumbaki (priest) to ask for blessings, good harvests, or healing. These ceremonies show their connection to unseen spirits believed to protect the land and people. The famous Hudhud chants, sung by Ifugao women during harvest or funerals, preserve their oral history and moral lessons — recognized by UNESCO as an Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity.
